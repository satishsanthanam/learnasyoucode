Credits: 38 Biases in 13 minutes

 

Bias Explanation How to Counter
Ostrich Effect Ignoring negative information or feedback. Regularly review your situation, even if uncomfortable.
Inability to Close Doors Difficulty in making decisions due to fear of missing out on other opportunities. Focus on one task for better results.
Contrast Effect Judgment of one thing is affected by recent exposure to something else. Be aware of how prior exposure influences your judgment.
Chauffeur Knowledge Appearing knowledgeable without genuine expertise. Ask deeper questions to identify true expertise.
Ikea Effect Valuing something more because you put effort into creating it. Seek external feedback to avoid overvaluing your work.
Curse of Specificity Extremely detailed information seems more persuasive, even if irrelevant. Be critical of overly detailed information that may be irrelevant.
Spotlight Effect Overestimating how much others notice your appearance or mistakes. Remember others are usually focused on themselves.
Halo Effect One positive attribute influences overall judgment. Judge each situation on its own merits.
Reciprocity Feeling obliged to return a favor. Recognize when actions are driven by obligation rather than genuine desire.
Self-Serving Bias Crediting oneself for successes but blaming external factors for failures. Practice accountability for both successes and failures.
Diderot Effect Acquiring a new possession leads to a spiral of additional purchases. Be mindful of how one purchase influences overall spending.
Anchoring Effect Initial information shapes subsequent thinking. View offers independently to make objective decisions.
Negativity Bias Focusing more on negative events than positive ones. Consciously shift focus to positive events and practice gratitude.
Sunk Cost Fallacy Continuing an endeavor due to past investments rather than current benefits. Make decisions based on potential future returns.
Paradox of Choice Too many options make decision-making harder. Limit options to simplify decisions.
Framing Effect Presentation of information influences decisions. Consider facts from multiple perspectives.
End of History Illusion Belief that one's current self will remain unchanged. Embrace change and recognize evolving preferences and beliefs.
Pygmalion Effect Higher expectations lead to improved performance. Set ambitious goals to enhance performance.
Consistency Principle Desire to align actions with previous commitments. Use accountability partners to achieve goals.
Planning Fallacy Underestimating how long tasks will take. Add extra time to plans to reduce stress.
Confirmation Bias Noticing information that supports existing beliefs. Actively seek information that challenges these beliefs.
Bandwagon Effect Following the crowd. Ask if a decision is genuinely desired or just following others.
Dunning-Kruger Effect Less skilled individuals overestimate their abilities. Consult experts for important decisions.
Loss Aversion Fear of losing something outweighs potential for gain. Focus on potential gains rather than just losses.
Decoy Effect Preferences shift due to the introduction of a third, less attractive option. Evaluate each option independently.
Availability Heuristic Judging the likelihood of events based on how easily they come to mind. Look for actual facts and statistics.
Gambler's Fallacy Believing past events affect future ones in random situations. Recognize the independence of each event.
Hindsight Bias Belief that one predicted an event after it occurred. Document your predictions and consider alternative outcomes.
Reactance Bias Wanting to do the opposite of what one is told. Focus on the benefits of the action.
Action Bias Pressure to act even when inaction might be wiser. Consider the benefits of patience.
Survivorship Bias Focusing on successes and ignoring failures. Research both successes and failures.
Unity Principle Being more persuaded by those perceived as part of one's group. Make decisions based on information, not the presenter.
Zeigarnik Effect Remembering uncompleted tasks better than completed ones. Plan the next day in advance to leverage this effect.
Bystander Effect Less likely to help when others are present. Address requests to specific individuals.
Ambiguity Effect Avoiding unclear choices, even if they might lead to better results. Assess risks and benefits of ambiguous options.
Curse of Knowledge Assuming others know what you know, leading to unclear communication. Explain concepts as if to a beginner.
Illusion of Averages Mistaken belief that average numbers always reveal the full story. Dig deeper into data for accurate understanding.
Endowment Effect Valuing something more just because you own it. See things from an outsider's perspective.
